The autocorrelation structure of the Beijing PM2.5 series exhibits a slow decay, remaining positive (approximately 0.15) even at lag 48, which indicates substantial temporal persistence. This persistence implies that naive last-value forecasts are difficult to outperform over a broad range of lead times, so persistence baselines remain highly competitive across many horizons. Consequently, several candidate models do not attain positive Skill(h) at short and medium horizons, and apparent gains should be interpreted relative to this strong autocorrelation-driven baseline.
