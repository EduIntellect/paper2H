In the PM2.5 Beijing experiment, the currently reported predictability horizon follows the original definition, \(H^*=\max\{h:\mathrm{Skill}(h)>0\}\), yielding \(H^*=48\). This estimate should be interpreted cautiously because Skill is negative at earlier horizons and becomes positive only at long lead times, a pattern consistent with late-horizon recovery rather than a contiguous interval of useful predictability. For this reason, a first-zero-crossing criterion is a plausible and potentially more robust operational estimator of the effective predictability limit.

The evaluation pipeline is leakage-free by construction: forecasts are generated in time order without random splits, horizon-specific targets are defined as `shift(-h)`, and baseline/model errors are computed on identical valid timestamps using a shared mask. This alignment enforces fair, temporally consistent comparison across horizons.
