In the Beijing PM2.5 experiment, the moving-average model exhibits negative Skill(h) across short and intermediate lead times (h=1–35), but becomes positive at longer horizons (h=36–48). This pattern indicates that persistence dominates while short-term memory is strong, yet its advantage diminishes as horizon increases and local persistence weakens. Under these conditions, smoothing-based forecasts can produce lower aggregate error than persistence, leading to a late-horizon skill recovery that is relevant for discussion of horizon-dependent model utility.
